Event Structure: Organizer + Participant Perspectives

Participant View: The Experience

Participants are handed a Google Colab Notebook containing:

  • A mystery dataset with partial/noisy column names and decoy data
  • A Bingo Grid of ML micro-tasks (e.g., normalization, encoding, train-test split)
  • Submission guidelines and scratchpad space

Their mission:

  • Solve rows/columns in the Bingo Grid to score points
  • Simultaneously deduce the purpose of the dataset

üîç Participant Journey: Step-by-Step Tactics & Learning Gains

This section delves deeper into the practical strategies participants employ and the invaluable machine learning understanding they gain at each phase of the "Data Detectives & ML Bingo" challenge.

üß≠ 1. First Contact with the Dataset

Upon opening the Colab notebook, participants encounter:

  • A dataset with cryptic column names (e.g., A, B, C)
  • A few clearly noisy or irrelevant columns (e.g., uniform values, random strings)
  • A Bingo grid of foundational ML tasks

What They Do:

  • Scan data types and distributions using `.info()`, `.describe()`, `.value_counts()`
  • Try basic visualizations (histograms, pairplots) to spot patterns

What They Learn:

  • How to profile a dataset from scratch
  • The importance of data types, nulls, and variance
  • Early exposure to data noise and real-world messiness

üéØ 2. Tackling Initial Bingo Tasks

They begin solving tasks like:

  • Normalization: Scaling numerical columns to a 0‚Äì1 range
  • One-hot encoding: Transforming hidden categorical columns
  • Missing values: Identifying incomplete or unhelpful columns

What They Do:

  • Use `MinMaxScaler`, `pd.get_dummies()`, `.isnull().sum()`
  • Drop, impute, or highlight problematic columns

What They Learn:

  • Preprocessing essentials
  • Importance of clean inputs for ML
  • Detecting and ignoring decoy data ‚Äî a key skill in real projects

üß™ 3. Experimentation & Exploration

As they progress:

  • They try creating correlation heatmaps to identify predictive links
  • Attempt simple Logistic Regression or Decision Tree models
  • Evaluate model using confusion matrix, accuracy score, etc.

What They Do:

  • Test if feature A or C helps predict label E
  • Remove low-importance columns
  • Adjust splits and re-test

What They Learn:

  • Role of feature importance in modeling
  • How model metrics reflect data quality and relevance
  • That data science involves iterative hypothesis testing

üîê 4. Cracking the Dataset Mystery

Through this process, participants begin connecting dots:

  • "Column A might be income ‚Äî values are in lakhs"
  • "Column E must be binary ‚Äî our classifier works well on it"
  • "Decoy column F has no variance ‚Äî safe to ignore"

They now write a reasoned guess:

‚ÄúWe believe this is a loan approval dataset where income, job type, and credit score predict whether a loan is granted (column E).‚Äù

What They Learn:

  • Data storytelling ‚Äî making sense of raw numbers
  • How ML isn't just coding, but also interpretation
  • Confidence in drawing conclusions from ambiguous data

üß† 5. Reflection & Team Learning

At the end, teams submit:

  • Their completed notebook
  • Reasoning behind dataset purpose
  • Any insights or challenges faced

Some teams may be right, others partially right, but all will have:

  • Practiced the full ML lifecycle ‚Äî from loading data to drawing conclusions
  • Learned to collaborate under uncertainty
  • Built intuition about datasets through structured exploration
